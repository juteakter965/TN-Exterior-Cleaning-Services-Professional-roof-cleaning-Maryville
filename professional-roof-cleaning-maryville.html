Why Pressure Washing Is Not Professional

Industry Standards: Why Professionals Don't Pressure Wash Roofs

  • Manufacturer Prohibition: All major shingle manufacturers prohibit high-pressure washing
  • Warranty Voidance: Pressure washing voids most roof manufacturer warranties
  • Shingle Damage: High pressure strips protective granules, reducing roof lifespan by years
  • Water Intrusion: Pressure forces water under shingles, causing leaks and damage
  • Incomplete Cleaning: Only cleans surface without killing algae/mold at root level
  • Professional Standards: Industry organizations explicitly recommend against pressure washing roofs

Bottom Line: Any service using high-pressure washing on roofs is NOT professional, regardless of their claims

Is professional soft wash cleaning really necessary, or will pressure washing work?

Professional soft wash cleaning is absolutely necessary - pressure washing is specifically prohibited by roofing manufacturers and industry standards. Here's why: (1) All major shingle manufacturers (GAF, CertainTeed, Owens Corning, etc.) explicitly prohibit high-pressure washing and void warranties if used, (2) High pressure (1,500-3,000+ PSI) strips protective granules from shingles, dramatically reducing roof lifespan, (3) Pressure forces water under shingles causing leaks, water damage, and mold growth, (4) Pressure washing only cleans the surface without killing algae/mold at root level, resulting in rapid regrowth, (5) Industry organizations (RCIA, ARMA, PWNA) all recommend against pressure washing roofs, and (6) True professionals never pressure wash roofs - it's a clear sign of amateur, non-professional service. Professional soft wash uses low pressure (under 500 PSI) with professional cleaning solutions that kill growth at the root, delivering results lasting 2-3 years while protecting your roof investment.

Key professional certifications include: (1) RCIA (Roof Cleaning Institute of America) certification demonstrating specialized roof cleaning training and adherence to industry standards, (2) PWNA (Power Washers of North America) membership indicating professional industry involvement, (3) Soft Wash Systems certification proving training in proper soft wash methodology, (4) OSHA safety certification for working at heights and chemical handling, (5) EPA compliance training for environmental protection and runoff management, (6) Manufacturer training from cleaning solution providers (like SoftWash Systems, etc.), and (7) State-specific environmental certifications for chemical use.
